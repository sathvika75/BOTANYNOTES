Meiosis


Prophase

                 Prophase is the initial phase of mitosis and meiosis, a period of cell division where

                chromosomes condense and become visible, and the mitotic spindle begins to form. During

                prophase DNA, which was diffuse chromatin during interphase, condenses into distinct chromosome.

                Key events during prophase:

                Chromatin Condensation:
                The genetic material, initially in a loosely organized form called chromatin, condenses and coils up,

                becoming visible as individual chromosomes. 

                Sister Chromatid Formation:
                Each chromosome is composed of two identical sister chromatids, joined at the centromere, giving them

                an X-shaped appearance. 

                Mitotic Spindle Formation:
                The mitotic spindle, which is composed of microtubules, begins to form from the centrosomes (or poles 

                in plant cells). 

                Nuclear Envelope Breakdown:
                The nuclear envelope, which surrounds the nucleus during interphase, begins to break down.

Metaphase

                         Metaphase is a crucial stage in cell division (mitosis and meiosis) where chromosomes, 

                         which have already replicated and condensed, are aligned at the center of the cell. 

                         This alignment ensures the during the subsequent phase (anaphase), 

                         each daughter cell receives a complete and equal set of chromosomes.

                         Key Features of Metaphase:

                         Condensed Chromosomes:
                         Chromosomes are at their most condensed state, making them easily visible under a microscope.

                         Nuclear Envelope Disintegration:
                         The nuclear envelope, which normally surrounds the nucleus, breaks down, allowing the chromosomes

                         to move freely in the cytoplasm. 

                         Chromosome Alignment:
                         Sister chromatids (identical copies of a chromosome joined at the centromere) are aligned at the 

                         metaphase plate (an imaginary line in the center of the cell). 

                         Metaphase Checkpoint:
                         A checkpoint ensures that all chromosomes are properly attached to the spindle microtubules before

                         the cell progresses to anaphase.

Anaphase

                       Anaphase is the stage of cell division where replicated chromosomes, called sister chromatids,

                       separate and move to opposite poles of the cell. This occurs after metaphase and precedes telophase

                       in both mitosis and meiosis. The separation of chromatids is crucial for ensuring each daughter cell

                       receives a complete and identical set of chromosomes.

                       Key Events in Anaphase:

                       Chromatid Separation:
                       The centromeres holding sister chromatids together divide, and each chromatid becomes an independent

                       chromosome.

                       Chromosome Movement:
                       The mitotic spindle, composed of microtubules, pulls the separated chromosomes towards opposite poles of

                       the cell.

                       Two Phases (Anaphase A and Anaphase B): 
                       Anaphase A: The movement of chromosomes towards the spindle poles is facilitated by shortening

                       microtubules. 
                       Anaphase B: The two poles of the cell move further apart, further aiding in chromosome separation.

Telophase

                            Telophase, the final stage of mitosis and meiosis, marks the point where duplicated chromosomes, 

                            previously separated at the poles of the cell, are enclosed by new nuclear membranes, forming two 

                            distinct nuclei. It also involves the breakdown of the mitotic spindle and the decondensation of the

                            chromosomes, making them less visible. This stage is followed by cytokinesis, where the cell divides 

                            into two daughter cells. 

                            Key features of telophase:

                            Nuclear membrane reformation:
                            New nuclear envelopes form around each set of chromosomes, creating two separate nuclei within the 

                            cell. 

                            Spindle disassembly:
                            The spindle apparatus, responsible for chromosome movement, breaks down.

                            Chromosome decondensation:
                            The chromosomes, which were tightly coiled during earlier stages, uncoil and become less condensed,

                            returning to their "stringy" form.

                            Cytokinesis:
                            The cytoplasm divides, resulting in two separate daughter cells, typically following telophase.

                            Occurs in both mitosis and meiosis:
                            Telophase occurs in both mitosis, a process of cell division that creates two identical daughter 

                            cells, and meiosis, which results in the production of four non-identical daughter cells. In meiosis,

                            telophase occurs twice, as telophase I and telophase II.
